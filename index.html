Conditions We Support

Understanding GBS, CIDP & Variants

Learn about the neurological conditions at the heart of our mission

Guillain-Barré Syndrome (GBS)

Guillain-Barré Syndrome is a rare inflammatory disorder of the peripheral nerves characterized by rapid onset of weakness and, often, paralysis of the legs, arms, breathing muscles, and face.

GBS can affect anyone at any age, but it is more common in adults and males. It often follows a respiratory or gastrointestinal infection.

Common Symptoms

  • Weakness or tingling sensations in the legs
  • Weakness spreading to the upper body and arms
  • Difficulty walking or climbing stairs
  • Difficulty with facial movements
  • Severe lower back pain
  • Difficulty breathing
Important: GBS is a medical emergency. If you or someone you know experiences these symptoms, seek immediate medical attention.

Early diagnosis and treatment can significantly improve outcomes for GBS patients.

Chronic Inflammatory Demyelinating Polyneuropathy (CIDP)

CIDP is a rare neurological disorder characterized by gradually increasing sensory loss and weakness associated with loss of reflexes. Unlike GBS, CIDP develops over months rather than days or weeks.

Key Characteristics

  • Progressive weakness over at least 8 weeks
  • Numbness and tingling in hands and feet
  • Loss of reflexes
  • Fatigue and balance problems
  • Difficulty with fine motor skills

Treatment Options

CIDP is treatable, and many patients respond well to therapies including:

  • IVIG (Intravenous Immunoglobulin) - First-line treatment
  • Corticosteroids - Anti-inflammatory medication
  • Plasma Exchange - Removes harmful antibodies

With proper treatment, many CIDP patients can maintain good quality of life.

Multifocal Motor Neuropathy (MMN)

MMN is a rare immune-mediated neuropathy characterized by slowly progressive, asymmetric limb weakness without sensory loss. It primarily affects motor nerves.

Distinguishing Features

  • Weakness typically starts in the hands or feet
  • Asymmetric pattern (one side more than the other)
  • No sensory symptoms (numbness/tingling)
  • Muscle cramps and twitching
  • Foot drop or hand weakness
Good News: MMN typically responds very well to IVIG treatment, and most patients maintain good functional ability with ongoing therapy.

February is MMN Awareness Month - helping spread understanding of this condition.
